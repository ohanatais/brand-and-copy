# Core Principles

Loaded for every product-copy session. The evidence base and the stances that separate copy
that converts from copy that's invisible.

## The thesis: specific converts, generic is invisible

The reader's default is to ignore you. Generic copy — "the best way to manage your
workflow," "powerful tools for modern teams" — confirms the instinct to scroll past, because
it could be about anything and is therefore about nothing. Specific copy interrupts the
scroll because it sounds like it knows something true about *this* reader and *their*
situation. The entire craft is moving from generic to specific: a specific reader, a specific
moment, a specific problem, in specific words. Cleverness is optional; specificity is not.

## Framework 1 — Schwartz's five stages of awareness

From Eugene Schwartz's *Breakthrough Advertising* (1966) — the single most useful lens in
copywriting because it determines the *job of the opening line*. Before writing a word, place
the reader on this ladder:

1. **Unaware** — doesn't know they have the problem. The copy's job: name the problem so they
   feel it. (Leading with your product here fails — they don't know why they'd care.)
2. **Problem-aware** — feels the problem, doesn't know solutions exist. Job: show you
   understand the problem precisely, then reveal that a solution exists.
3. **Solution-aware** — knows solutions like yours exist, weighing options. Job: your
   **differentiation** — why this one, for them. (This is where positioning's "only" earns
   its keep.)
4. **Product-aware** — knows your product, not yet convinced. Job: proof, specifics,
   objection handling, the value made concrete.
5. **Most-aware** — ready, needs a nudge. Job: a clear offer and an easy next step; don't
   re-explain what they already know.

**The rule that prevents most failures:** match the copy to where the reader *actually is*,
which is usually one or two stages *earlier* than the founder assumes. The founder is Most-
aware about their own product; the visitor rarely is. Same product, same truth, but the
headline for an Unaware reader and a Product-aware reader share almost no words.

## Framework 2 — Jobs to be done: write to the struggle

People don't buy products; they hire them to make progress in a situation. JTBD reframes copy
away from *what the product is* toward *what the reader is trying to get done* and the
struggle that pushed them to look. Copy written to the job ("finally know which of your skills
actually set you apart") beats copy written to the feature ("AI-powered skills analysis")
because it speaks to the outcome the reader already wants. Find the job by asking what
happened right before someone went looking for a product like this.

## Framework 3 — Voice of customer: mine, don't invent

The best copy is *assembled, not authored* (the Copyhackers / Joanna Wiebe practice). The
words that convert are usually already out there — in app-store reviews, support tickets,
sales calls, Reddit threads, and customer interviews. "Message mining" means harvesting the
phrases real people use to describe the problem and the relief, then putting those phrases in
the copy. This does two things: it makes the copy specific and native automatically, and it
removes the founder's guesswork. Whenever the user has any real customer language, pull from
it before writing anything new. When they don't, write a hypothesis and flag that the
strongest version will come from real words later.

## Framework 4 — Features → benefits → meaning (the "so what" test)

A feature is what the product has. A benefit is what it does. *Meaning* is what that changes
in the reader's life — and meaning is what converts. Walk every important claim down the
ladder by asking "so what?" until you hit something the reader actually feels:

- Feature: "exports to PDF"
- → so what? Benefit: "share your work without sending the file"
- → so what? Meaning: "look professional to a client in two clicks, with nothing to install"

Run "so what?" at least twice on every benefit. Stop when the next "so what?" would be absurd
— that's the line worth writing.

## Structures are tools, not gospel

Classic copy structures help organize a message; use them, don't worship them:
- **PAS** — Problem, Agitate, Solve. Strong for Problem-aware readers.
- **AIDA** — Attention, Interest, Desire, Action. A general arc for a page or email.
- **The value-proposition formula** — what you do + for whom + the specific benefit/why-you.
- **Headline patterns** (the "4 U's": useful, urgent, unique, ultra-specific) — as a checklist
  to strengthen a headline, not a template to fill.

Reach for the structure that fits the reader's awareness stage and the asset, then make it
disappear behind natural language.

## Universal by design — derive from this reader

The frameworks are fixed; the copy must not be. The same method writes a calm, technical
landing page for a security tool and a loud, playful one for a party game — because each is
derived from *that* reader, *that* job, and *that* voice. Every example in these files shows
*how to decide*, never a phrase to reuse. If the copy starts sounding like a swipe file of
clever lines, stop and re-derive from the reader's awareness stage and their actual words. And
always write in the brand's defined voice (`/tone-of-voice`): great copy in the wrong voice
still breaks the brand.

## Clarity is the floor, not the ceiling

Before "is this persuasive?", ask "is this clear?" The **5-second test**: a stranger reading
only the hero should be able to say what this is and who it's for. If they can't, no amount of
persuasion downstream matters. Clarity, specificity, and the reader's own words do most of the
work; persuasion technique is the smaller part on top.
