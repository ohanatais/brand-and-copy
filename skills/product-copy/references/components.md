# Component Deep-Dives

The per-layer and per-asset method. Load when a session reaches the depth of an actual layer
or asset. Examples use three *contrasting* products so the method is visible without implying
one style is correct:

- **Product A — a consumer habit app** (warm voice; reader often Problem-aware).
- **Product B — a B2B security tool** (precise voice; reader often Solution/Product-aware).
- **Product C — a niche creator tool** (irreverent voice; reader often Unaware of the
  specific approach).

The user's product is its own thing — derive its copy, don't borrow these lines.

## 1. Reader & moment

**Goal:** name the reader and their awareness stage before writing, because it sets the job
of the opening.

**Method:**
1. Identify *which* reader and *where* they're hitting this asset (cold ad, organic search,
   pricing page, in-product). The traffic source is a strong clue to the awareness stage.
2. Place them on Schwartz's ladder (Unaware → Most-aware; see `core-principles.md`).
3. Write the **job of the opening** for that stage in one line. Everything else serves it.

| | Product A | Product B | Product C |
|---|---|---|---|
| Reader hits via | a problem they googled | a comparison/review | a curiosity post |
| Likely stage | Problem-aware | Solution-aware | Unaware (of this approach) |
| Job of the opening | name the problem precisely | show the differentiation | make them feel the problem exists |

## 2. Message: the value proposition

**Goal:** one sentence that means something — what this does, for whom, why it matters.

**Method:**
1. Anchor in the **job to be done** and the **differentiation** ("the only ___ that ___",
   from positioning if it exists).
2. Use a value-proposition formula as scaffolding, then make it human:
   *"[Product] helps [specific who] [achieve specific outcome] without [the pain of the
   alternative]."* Replace every generic slot with something specific.
3. Test it: would a competitor's page carry the exact same sentence? If yes, it's not a value
   proposition, it's a category description. Push until it's ownable.

**Weak → strong (illustrative, not to reuse):**
- Weak: "The smart way to manage your habits." (could be any habit app)
- Stronger: "See the streak you didn't know you had — and the one quietly slipping."
  (specific, names a real outcome, sounds like Product A's warm voice)

The value proposition is the seed; the headline, subhead, and CTA are all expressions of it.

## 3. Structure: the anatomy of the asset

Each asset has a job per part. Use these as a **checklist, not a cage** — drop parts the
reader's stage doesn't need (a Most-aware reader doesn't need the problem re-explained).

### Landing page
The job of each section:
- **Hero** — the value proposition made vivid: a headline that does work, a subhead that adds
  the specific, one primary CTA. The 5-second test lives here.
- **The problem / the stakes** — for colder readers: show you understand the problem better
  than they could say it themselves.
- **How it works** — make the mechanism concrete in 3 steps; remove the fear of "will I get
  this?"
- **Proof** — evidence the claim is true: specifics, results, testimonials in the customer's
  own words, logos if real.
- **Objections** — answer the top 2–3 reasons they'd hesitate, before they have to.
- **Final CTA** — restate the value and ask once more, now that the work is done.

### Onboarding copy (the activation arc)
- The first five sentences decide whether they stay. Speak to the value immediately, in the
  voice; don't open with a tour.
- Reduce every step to the next single action. Each screen: one job, one sentence of why,
  one button.
- Celebrate the first real win in the product's voice — the moment they feel the value is the
  moment to name it.

### Email (one goal, one CTA)
- A subject line that earns the open (specific, not clickbait), an opening that respects the
  reader's time, one clear ask, one CTA. Cut everything that doesn't serve the single goal.

| Asset | Product A | Product B | Product C |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hero job | name the slipping habit | prove it's safer *and* faster | provoke "wait, that's a thing?" |
| Proof that fits | a relatable before/after | a benchmark + a named customer | a surprising demo |

## 4. Craft: lines that work

**Goal:** every line earns its place. Three moves, applied relentlessly:

1. **Specificity.** Replace abstractions with concretes. Numbers, named situations, real
   nouns. "Save time" → "cut your Monday reporting from an hour to five minutes." Specific is
   believable; vague is forgettable.
2. **Benefits with meaning** — run the "so what?" ladder (feature → benefit → meaning) at
   least twice on every claim. Write the line at the bottom of the ladder, the one the reader
   actually feels.
3. **Voice of customer** — use the reader's own phrases (mined from reviews, tickets,
   interviews). When you have real language, prefer it to anything invented; it's specific and
   native for free.

All of it in the **defined brand voice** (`/tone-of-voice`). The same benefit reads
differently in a warm voice and a blunt one — and it must match, or the page feels written by
a committee.

**The "so what" ladder in practice (Product B):**
- Feature: "agentless deployment"
- → so what? "nothing to install on every machine"
- → so what? "you're protected by this afternoon, not next quarter" ← write this one

## 5. Proof & CTA

**Goal:** earn belief, then make the next step feel inevitable.

**Objections — answer before they're voiced.**
1. List the top 2–3 reasons this reader would *not* act (too expensive, too hard to switch,
   won't work for my case, don't trust it yet).
2. Address each directly in the copy — not defensively, just answered. An FAQ, a comparison,
   a guarantee, a "works even if…" line. The reader feels understood, not sold to.

**Proof — back every claim.** Match proof to the claim: a speed claim wants a number; a
trust claim wants a testimonial or a name; an ease claim wants a 3-step demo. Specific proof
in the customer's words beats adjectives ("amazing", "powerful") every time.

**CTA — the obvious next step.**
- **Specific verb tied to the value**, not generic. "Start my streak" / "See my report" beats
  "Sign up" / "Submit."
- **Low friction** — reduce the perceived cost of clicking (free, no card, takes a minute);
  say it next to the button.
- **One primary CTA per asset.** Competing CTAs split the decision and convert worse.
- A CTA feels *inevitable* when the copy above already did the persuading — by the time the
  reader reaches it, clicking should feel like the natural conclusion, not a leap.

| | Product A | Product B | Product C |
|---|---|---|---|
| Top objection | "I've failed at habit apps before" | "switching is risky" | "I don't need another tool" |
| Answer | "no streaks to lose — start fresh anytime" | "runs alongside what you have; remove in one click" | "it's a 30-second thing, not a workflow" |
| CTA | "See my first streak" | "Run a free scan" | "Try the weird version" |

Always produce **2–3 CTA/headline variations** to test — copy is a hypothesis, and the reader
is the only judge that counts.
